Understanding Alzheimer's Disease

Progressive neurological disorder that causes brain cells to die and the brain to shrink (atrophy)
Most common cause of dementia — a continuous decline in thinking, behavioral and social skills
Early signs include forgetting recent events or conversations
As the disease progresses, patients develop severe memory impairment and lose ability to carry out everyday tasks
Treatments may temporarily improve symptoms but there is currently no cure

Comparison of a healthy brain (left) and a brain affected by Alzheimer's disease (right)

Alzheimer's Disease: By the Numbers

Alzheimer's disease is a growing public health crisis affecting millions of Americans and their families:

Prevalence

An estimated 6.9 million Americans age 65 and older are living with Alzheimer's in 2025. This number could grow to 13.8 million by 2060 without medical breakthroughs.

Demographics

1 in 9 people age 65 and older (10.9%) has Alzheimer's. Almost two-thirds of Americans with Alzheimer's are women, and older Black Americans are about twice as likely to have Alzheimer's as older White Americans.

Impact

Alzheimer's is the 6th leading cause of death in the United States. In 2023, Alzheimer's and other dementias cost the nation $345 billion, with this number expected to rise to nearly $1 trillion by 2050.

Brain Changes in Alzheimer's Disease

Alzheimer's disease is characterized by specific changes in the brain:

Amyloid plaques: Abnormal clusters of protein fragments that build up between nerve cells
Neurofibrillary tangles: Abnormal collections of the protein tau that accumulate inside neurons
Loss of connections: Damage to neuronal connections and communication between networks of neurons
Inflammation: Activation of immune system cells (microglia) in response to brain damage
Brain atrophy: Widespread brain cell death leading to significant tissue shrinkage

The changes begin years before symptoms appear, with damage initially occurring in regions involved in memory formation

Symptoms and Disease Progression

Alzheimer's typically progresses slowly through three general stages: early, middle and late stage. The symptoms become more severe over time:

Early Stage

  • Memory lapses (recent conversations, events)
  • Difficulty finding the right word
  • Trouble organizing or planning
  • Getting lost in familiar places
  • Making poor judgments

Middle Stage

  • Increasing memory loss and confusion
  • Difficulty recognizing family and friends
  • Inability to learn new things
  • Problems with language and reading
  • Hallucinations and paranoia
  • Impulsive behavior

Late Stage

  • Inability to communicate
  • Complete dependence on caregivers
  • Loss of physical abilities (walking, sitting)
  • Difficulty swallowing
  • Loss of awareness of surroundings
  • Vulnerability to infections

Risk Factors

Non-modifiable Risk Factors

Age: The greatest known risk factor; most people with Alzheimer's are 65 and older
Family history: Having a first-degree relative with Alzheimer's increases risk
Genetics: Specific genes (APOE-e4) increase risk
Down syndrome: Many people with Down syndrome develop Alzheimer's as they age

Modifiable Risk Factors

Cardiovascular health: Heart disease, high blood pressure, high cholesterol
Diabetes: Type 2 diabetes may increase risk
Head injuries: Severe or repeated traumatic brain injuries
Lifestyle factors: Poor diet, lack of exercise, smoking, excessive alcohol use
Education level: Lower levels of education are associated with increased risk

Diagnosing Alzheimer's Disease

There is no single test that can definitively diagnose Alzheimer's disease. Diagnosis typically involves:

Medical history: Detailed review of symptoms, previous medical issues, and family history
Cognitive tests: Mental status testing to evaluate memory, problem-solving, attention, and language
Physical examination: To rule out other conditions that may cause similar symptoms
Neurological exams: Evaluating reflexes, muscle tone, speech, and coordination
Brain imaging: MRI, CT, or PET scans to identify structural changes or rule out other conditions

PET scans can detect amyloid plaques and tau tangles in the brain, which are hallmarks of Alzheimer's disease

Emerging Diagnostic Tools

Blood tests that can detect biomarkers of Alzheimer's disease are being developed, which may enable earlier and less invasive diagnosis in the future.

Current Treatments

While there's no cure for Alzheimer's disease, several medications and non-drug approaches can help manage symptoms:

Medications for Cognitive Symptoms

  • Cholinesterase inhibitors (Donepezil, Rivastigmine, Galantamine): Help preserve a chemical messenger involved in memory and thinking
  • Memantine: Regulates glutamate, another brain chemical involved in learning and memory
  • Combination therapy: Some patients benefit from combining memantine with a cholinesterase inhibitor

Disease-Modifying Treatments

  • Lecanemab (Leqembi): FDA-approved immunotherapy drug targeting beta-amyloid for early Alzheimer's
  • Donanemab (Kisunla): FDA-approved for patients with mild cognitive impairment or mild dementia due to Alzheimer's

Non-Drug Approaches

  • Cognitive stimulation: Activities to maintain mental function
  • Environmental modifications: Creating a safe, structured environment
  • Behavioral strategies: Techniques to manage challenging behaviors
  • Physical activity: Regular exercise can help maintain physical function

Emerging Treatments & Research

Research into new Alzheimer's treatments is targeting several approaches:

Anti-amyloid therapies: Additional antibodies that target and clear amyloid plaques
Anti-tau approaches: Treatments to prevent the formation of tau tangles
Anti-inflammatory drugs: Reducing the brain inflammation associated with Alzheimer's
Neuroprotection: Protecting brain cells from damage and death
Gene therapy: Addressing genetic risk factors for Alzheimer's
Stem cell therapy: Potential to replace damaged brain cells

Current Clinical Trials

Hundreds of clinical trials are currently investigating potential Alzheimer's treatments at various phases of development.

These include therapies targeting multiple aspects of the disease, from amyloid and tau to inflammation, metabolic dysfunction, and neuronal protection.

Prevention Strategies

While there's no proven way to prevent Alzheimer's disease, research suggests several strategies may help reduce risk:

Cardiovascular Health

Manage blood pressure, cholesterol, and diabetes; avoid smoking; maintain a healthy weight

Healthy Diet

Mediterranean or MIND diet rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, fish, and olive oil; low in red meat and processed foods

Physical Activity

Regular exercise may directly benefit brain cells by increasing blood and oxygen flow to the brain

Cognitive Stimulation

Mentally stimulating activities, education, and learning new skills may build cognitive reserve

Social Engagement

Maintaining social connections and participating in social activities may support brain health

Impact on Caregivers

Alzheimer's disease affects not only those with the condition but also their caregivers and families:

Emotional Burden

59% of dementia caregivers report high to very high emotional stress, with 30-40% experiencing depression

Financial Impact

Caregivers spend an average of $12,388 annually in out-of-pocket costs related to caregiving

Time Commitment

In 2023, 11.5 million family caregivers provided an estimated 18.4 billion hours of unpaid care

Supporting Caregivers

Education and Training

Understanding Alzheimer's progression and learning care techniques can reduce stress and improve care quality

Support Groups

Connecting with others in similar situations provides emotional support and practical advice

Respite Care

Short-term relief for caregivers helps prevent burnout and maintains caregiver health

Self-Care Practices

Encouraging caregivers to maintain their own health and well-being is essential

Our Mission

Our goal is not only to spread awareness of this disease, but also to contribute to the fight against Alzheimer's Disease. As a strong community, we wish to understand, combat, and overcome this disease through service, innovation, and creativity.

Founded in Spring Semester 2015, Buckeyes Against Alzheimer's is an undergraduate organization at The Ohio State University devoted to:

  • Promoting awareness about Alzheimer's disease and its prevention
  • Educating our community about the impacts of Alzheimer's
  • Serving those affected by Alzheimer's through regular volunteering
  • Supporting research efforts to find treatments and a cure

Our Activities

  • Weekly volunteering at local care facilities
  • Educational seminars and presentations
  • Hosting speakers and experts in the field
  • Awareness events and fundraising campaigns
  • Bi-weekly club meetings to develop initiatives
